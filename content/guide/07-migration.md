# Do Great Blue Herons Migrate? The Story of a Partial Migrant

Yes and no — the Great Blue Heron is a classic **partial migrant**. Populations in Florida, along the Pacific coast from Washington to Alaska, and in the Galápagos stay put year-round, while birds breeding east of the Rockies in Canada and the northern United States move south as waters freeze.[^1^][^2^] Cornell's range guidance summarizes it simply: herons "generally move away from the northern edge of their breeding range in winter, with some flying as far south as the Caribbean."[^3^]

## The rules of movement

- **What triggers migration**: food, not temperature. Herons tolerate real cold, but they cannot hunt through ice. Once lakes, marshes, and snow-covered fields lock up, the birds leave; where open water persists, they may stay all winter even in the north.[^1^]
- **Fall timing**: generally mid-September to late October, later (November–December) in Alberta and Ontario.[^1^]
- **Spring timing**: returns run February through April, earliest in the Midwest and South; central and coastal Canadian breeders may not return until mid-May.[^1^]
- **How far**: from a few hundred miles to more than **3,000 miles**, with some birds wintering in Guatemala, the Caribbean, or northern South America.[^1^]
- **How they travel**: alone or in small groups of about 3–12 (occasionally ~100), by day and by night.[^1^]

## Harper's marathon: what GPS tracking revealed

In 2019, Maine biologists GPS-tagged an adult female heron named "Harper" in Harpswell. After summering on Québec's Chaleur Bay, she departed on October 3 and rewrote expectations: a **nonstop, 1,424-mile (2,291 km) open-ocean flight** from Saint John, New Brunswick to Nocatee, Florida in **38.6 hours**, averaging **38 mph** (max 67 mph) at an average altitude of about 607 ft (max 3,103 ft). She then island-hopped the Bahamas and settled in Cuba, completing a **2,488-mile** journey in eight days. Accelerometer data confirmed she was flapping/flying the entire ocean crossing — not riding a ship.[^4^]

Harper wasn't typical in route but her numbers fit a broader dataset: five GPS-tagged Maine herons averaged **24 mph** in routine flight (range 9–60 mph) and typically flew around **164 ft** above ground.[^4^] Tracking also explains winter loyalty: a North Carolina male called "Big Blue" nested and wintered within the same 13 square miles of Lake Norman for six consecutive years — proof that where ice-free foraging exists, migration simply isn't necessary.[^4^]

## Western exceptionalism

West of the Rockies, even birds from the Alaska coastline generally do not migrate. Utah's Bear River Migratory Bird Refuge hosts herons all winter; some individuals there have learned to pirate or scavenge fish caught by Common Goldeneyes in tiny pockets of open water — a behavioral workaround for frozen wetlands.[^5^] Pacific *fannini* herons in British Columbia shift seasonally between intertidal foraging and winter grassland rodent hunting rather than migrating.[^6^]

## Migration fast facts

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Do all Great Blue Herons migrate? | No — only northern/eastern populations; it's a partial migrant[^1^] |
| Longest documented nonstop flight | 1,424 mi over open ocean, 38.6 hrs ("Harper," 2019)[^4^] |
| Typical migration speed | Avg 38 mph on marathon legs; ~24 mph routine[^4^] |
| Flock size on migration | Usually solo or 3–12 birds[^1^] |
| Winter destinations | Southern U.S., Mexico, Central America, Caribbean, N. South America[^1^][^3^] |
| Year-round regions | Florida, Gulf & Pacific coasts (incl. S. Alaska), Galápagos[^1^] |
