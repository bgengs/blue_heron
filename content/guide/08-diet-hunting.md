# What Do Great Blue Herons Eat? Diet and Hunting Strategy

Short answer: nearly anything within striking distance. The Great Blue Heron is an opportunistic apex predator of wetlands — fish are the mainstay, but the full menu runs to amphibians, reptiles, crustaceans, insects, small mammals, and other birds.[^1^][^2^]

## The menu

| Prey group | Examples documented |
|---|---|
| Fish (staple) | Flounder, sticklebacks, gunnels, sculpins, perch, shiner perch, smelt, eulachon, bass, top smelt, sunfish, bowfin; typically 10–20 cm, but up to 60 cm / ~900 g[^3^] |
| Amphibians | Leopard frogs, bullfrogs, toads, salamanders, amphiumas[^3^] |
| Reptiles | Small turtles (incl. hatchling snappers), snakes up to 105 cm water snakes, lizards[^3^] |
| Crustaceans | Crayfish, shrimp, swimmer crabs[^3^] |
| Insects | Grasshoppers, dragonflies, aquatic insects[^3^] |
| Mammals | Voles, shrews, rats, mice, ground squirrels, gophers, moles, muskrat, even weasels and cottontail rabbits[^3^] |
| Birds | Ducklings, rails, phalaropes, American dipper, pied-billed grebe, marsh tern chicks[^3^] |

Regional studies show how completely the diet tracks local abundance: in **Nova Scotia, 98% of the diet was flounder**; California herons take roughly 75% fish (mostly species not sought by anglers); British Columbia birds key on sticklebacks, gunnels, sculpins, and perch; and in one **Idaho study, voles made up 24–40% of the diet**, with muskrat and weasel remains in pellets.[^3^][^4^]

## How they hunt

The method is patience perfected. A hunting heron wades with slow, deliberate steps or stands absolutely motionless — often in water less than a foot deep, sometimes belly-deep — peering into the water until prey comes within range, then straightening its S-coiled neck in a strike so fast it registers as instantaneous to the human eye.[^1^][^5^] Small prey is grabbed in the mandibles; large fish may be **impaled** on the bill, then shaken to break or relax the sharp spines before being swallowed whole, head-first.[^6^] Specialized neck vertebrae make the coiled, spring-loaded strike possible, and a high density of rod photoreceptors lets herons hunt at night as effectively as by day — one of the few members of their genus to do so.[^7^][^8^]

Hunting is not limited to water. Herons stalk voles and gophers through fields, snatch rails at marsh edges, perch on floating kelp beds waiting for fish, and stand at commercial fish operations where scraps accumulate.[^1^][^8^] They may hunt from floating objects and shoreline perches as well.[^9^]

## How much do they eat?

An adult consumes roughly **13 ounces of fish per day** — about three 6-inch koi or ten 2-inch goldfish — and can double that intake when feeding chicks.[^10^] A breeding pair with two chicks burns about 2,000 calories a day as a family, which keeps both parents foraging almost constantly; chicks gain nearly 40 times their hatch weight (about 50 g to about 2 kg) in eight weeks.[^11^]

## Risks of gluttony

Their ambition occasionally outruns their anatomy. Herons swallow prey whole and can manage surprisingly wide items, but there are documented cases of birds **choking to death on oversized prey** — including deaths caused by attempting to swallow Pacific lamprey.[^12^][^3^]
