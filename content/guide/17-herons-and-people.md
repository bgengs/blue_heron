# Great Blue Herons and People: Conflict, Protection, and Partnership

## From target to protected icon

In the 19th century, herons and egrets were slaughtered for the millinery trade — by one 1886 estimate, 50 North American bird species were being killed for feathers, and blue herons were among the targets.[^1^][^2^] The backlash created modern bird conservation: Boston socialites Harriet Hemenway and Minna Hall organized a boycott of feathered hats in 1896, recruiting 900 women and founding the Massachusetts Audubon Society — the seed of the National Audubon Society.[^2^] Their campaign drove the Lacey Act (1900), the Audubon Model Law (1901), the Weeks-McLean Act (1913), and finally the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918**, which ended the plume trade and still protects Great Blue Herons today: it is illegal to capture, harm, or possess the birds, their nests, or their eggs.[^2^][^3^]

## The fish-pond conflict

The most common modern friction is backyard and commercial fish. A heron can eat about 13 ounces of fish a day — three 6-inch koi or ten 2-inch goldfish — and doubles that while feeding chicks, so an ornamental pond is an irresistible buffet.[^4^] Humane, effective deterrents:

- **Give fish hiding places** — a length of drain pipe on the pond bottom works.[^5^]
- **Overhead barriers** — grid netting or crisscrossed silver mylar tape on 18-inch stakes, twisted to flash in the breeze.[^4^][^6^]
- **Dogs and motion-activated deterrents** at aquaculture facilities.[^7^]
- **Hazing** — repeatedly walking out and shouting; most herons get the message.[^6^]

Context worth sharing: at fish hatcheries, one study found herons took **mostly diseased fish that would have died shortly anyway** — the sick fish lingered near the surface where herons could catch them.[^8^] And remember the legal frame: because of the MBTA, any active management of herons requires consulting wildlife authorities first.[^7^]

## Herons as neighbors and city birds

Herons habituate remarkably well to settled landscapes: they fish drainage ditches and golf-course ponds, and urban colonies thrive in Vancouver's Stanley Park (nesting since 1921) and beside Seattle's Ballard Locks.[^9^][^10^] Seattle went furthest — on **March 17, 2003, the City Council designated the Great Blue Heron the official city bird** (Resolution 30586), citing its power to raise public awareness of wildlife habitat.[^11^] Grassroots stewardship followed: Heron Habitat Helpers, founded in 2001, won Seattle's first Wildlife Sanctuary designation for Kiwanis Ravine and secured nest-protection rules and $600,000 in restoration funding.[^10^]

## Herons as scientific partners

- **Sentinel species**: Environment and Climate Change Canada harvests heron eggs from St. Lawrence colonies every five years to track mercury, PCBs, DDE, and PBDEs — the heron is the official bioindicator of the river's health.[^12^]
- **Ecosystem services**: as apex predators they keep fish and small-animal populations in check and move nutrients; as colony nesters they fertilize rookery woodlands.[^13^]
- **Public science**: the Cornell Lab's Sapsucker Woods nest cam (live since 2012) has drawn millions of viewer-hours and thousands of schoolchildren into heron biology.[^14^]
- **Community monitoring**: programs like Audubon Florida's Colony Watch, Venice Rookery Watch (data since 1997), Stanley Park's Adopt-a-Nest, and Maine's Heron Observation Network let the public directly support science.[^15^][^16^]

## Living alongside herons: quick guidance

| Situation | Do this |
|---|---|
| Heron at your fish pond | Netting/mylar lines + fish hideouts; haze early and often[^4^][^6^] |
| Rookery on your land | No tree work or loud activity Feb–Jul within ~¼ mile; call your wildlife agency[^17^] |
| Watching a colony | ≥100 m distance, binoculars, quiet[^7^] |
| Injured heron | Never handle (the bill strikes at eyes); call a licensed rehabilitator[^18^] |
