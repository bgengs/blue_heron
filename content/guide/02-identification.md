# Great Blue Heron Identification: Size, Plumage, and Field Marks

The Great Blue Heron is one of the easiest large birds to identify in North America — as the Cornell Lab of Ornithology puts it, there is "no other similar species in range."[^1^] It is the largest of the North American herons, built on long legs, a sinuous neck, and a thick, daggerlike bill, with head, chest, and wing plumes that give adults a shaggy appearance.[^2^] In flight it curls its neck into a tight "S" shape, its broad, rounded wings beat slowly, and its legs trail well beyond the tail.[^2^]

## Measurements

| Measurement | Range (both sexes) |
|---|---|
| Length | 38.2–53.9 in (97–137 cm) |
| Weight | 74.1–88.2 oz (2,100–2,500 g) |
| Wingspan | 65.8–79.1 in (167–201 cm) |
| Standing height | Up to ~4–4.5 ft (115–138 cm)[^3^] |
| Bill | Long, straight, ~5–6 in[^4^] |

Figures from the Cornell Lab's All About Birds guide.[^2^] Regional studies add nuance: in British Columbia, adult males averaged 2.48 kg and females 2.11 kg; Nova Scotia/New England adults of both sexes averaged 2.23 kg; Oregon birds averaged 2.09 kg. The all-white "great white heron" population of south Florida is the heaviest, with males averaging 3.02 kg.[^5^] Despite the imposing frame, hollow bones keep the bird light enough to fly high and efficiently.[^6^]

## Plumage and color pattern

Adults are predominantly blue-gray above, with a mostly white head crossed by a wide black stripe that runs from above the eye to the back of the head, ending in short black plumes. The foreneck is white streaked with black; the shoulders show a black-and-chestnut pattern; and the bill is yellow to yellow-orange (dark above, yellow below).[^1^][^3^] During the breeding season the colors intensify — the bill and lores brighten, and adults grow long, showy occipital plumes and shaggy neck and chest plumes used in courtship displays.[^7^] Males and females look nearly identical, though males average larger with slightly longer ornamental plumes.[^8^]

## Telling age and sex

- **Juveniles** are duller and more uniformly gray than adults, with a solid dark crown (no white face framed by black), streaked necks, and no long head plumes.[^9^][^10^]
- Young birds acquire white streaking on the crown by their second fall and approach full adult plumage around their third year; they do not breed until at least two years old.[^8^][^11^]
- **Male vs. female** is best judged by size when a pair stands together: females average noticeably smaller with smaller bills; plumage differences are minimal.[^8^]

## Flight silhouette — the giveaway

Few sights are as diagnostic: broad, rounded wings with a two-toned look (paler leading edge, darker flight feathers), deep slow wingbeats, neck pulled back into a compact "S" with the head resting above the shoulders, and long legs streaming behind the tail.[^2^][^6^] The tucked neck instantly separates herons from cranes, which fly with necks outstretched.[^12^] Cruising speed is roughly 20–30 mph, and GPS-tracked birds in Maine averaged about 24 mph in everyday flight.[^6^][^13^]
