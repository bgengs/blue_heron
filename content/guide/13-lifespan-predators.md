# Great Blue Heron Lifespan, Mortality, and Predators

## How long do they live?

The average Great Blue Heron lives about **15 years** in the wild, and the longevity record from banding is **23–25 years**.[^1^][^2^] Those averages conceal a brutal first year: banding studies show roughly **67–71% of herons die within their first year of life**, after which annual mortality settles to about 29%.[^3^][^4^] In a typical nest, only about two chicks per brood survive to independence, lost to starvation, siblicide, falls, predators, and weather.[^5^] Survivors that make it through year one enter a long adult life — the average age of a breeding adult in British Columbia has been calculated at 5.6 years.[^6^]

## Who eats a Great Blue Heron?

Adults have few natural predators: a full-grown heron is large, wary, and armed with a dagger-like bill that makes it a genuinely dangerous target.[^7^] The documented exceptions:

| Predator | What it takes |
|---|---|
| **Bald Eagle** | Attacks herons at every life stage — eggs, chicks, juveniles, adults; the single most impactful predator at colonies[^7^][^8^] |
| Golden Eagle, Great Horned Owl | Occasionally take adults; one heron mortally wounded an attacking Golden Eagle before dying of its own injuries[^7^] |
| Bobcat | One documented kill of an adult heron[^7^] |
| Raccoon | Major egg and nestling predator; can cause complete colony failure, and many eggs[^7^][^9^] |
| American Crow, Common Raven, Turkey Vulture | Eggs and small nestlings[^7^] |
| Red-tailed Hawk, American Black Bear | Larger nestlings and fledglings[^7^] |
| American Alligator / Crocodile | Occasional adults or unsteady fledglings in the Southeast[^7^] |
| Harris's Hawk | One record of a young hawk killing a subadult heron[^7^] |

## The Bald Eagle problem

The recovery of the Bald Eagle — a conservation triumph — has become the Great Blue Heron's most pressing natural threat. Eagles raid colonies for eggs, chicks, and even adults, and their mere presence can trigger colony abandonment. On British Columbia's south coast, eagle attacks **more than doubled in frequency** as eagle populations grew from the 1980s onward.[^6^] In Maine, biologists attribute a **93% decline in nesting pairs on coastal islands since 1983** largely to the expanding eagle population.[^9^] Herons are not passive: colonies sometimes relocate en masse after sustained eagle pressure, and some urban colonies appear buffered by surrounding development that eagles avoid.[^8^]

## Why parents don't fight

Observers are often surprised that a heron will stand aside while an owl or eagle raids its nest. The logic is evolutionary: an adult heron's best strategy is to survive and breed again, not risk mortal injury defending replaceable eggs. There are exceptions — a filmed case shows a parent screaming at a Great Horned Owl until it departed — but restraint is the rule.[^5^]

## The top killer: us

For all the natural drama, the primary source of breeding failure at heronries is **human activity** — recreation, habitat destruction, and (historically) egg collectors and plume hunters. Repeated human intrusion into nesting areas routinely ends in abandonment of eggs or chicks.[^7^][^9^]
