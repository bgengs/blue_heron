# Great Blue Heron FAQ and Fun Facts

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Great Blue Herons endangered?**
No. The species is IUCN Least Concern with an increasing population (~700,000 breeders), though the Pacific *fannini* subspecies is of Special Concern in Canada, Florida's "great white heron" is Endangered on BirdLife's list, and Maine reports steep local declines.[^1^][^2^]

**Do Great Blue Herons mate for life?**
No — they are seasonally monogamous, choosing a new mate most years, though pairs that reunite at a colony may re-bond quickly.[^3^][^4^]

**How big are they, really?**
Up to 4.5 ft tall, 97–137 cm long, 167–201 cm wingspan — yet only about 5–6 lb, thanks to hollow bones.[^5^][^6^]

**Is it a crane?**
No. Cranes fly with necks outstretched, eat mostly plants, and can't perch in trees; herons fly with necks folded, eat animals, and nest in trees.[^7^]

**Can they hunt at night?**
Yes — rod-rich eyes give them excellent night vision; they are among the only members of their genus that forage after dark.[^8^]

**What do they eat besides fish?**
Frogs, snakes (up to ~3.5 ft water snakes), turtles, crayfish, crabs, insects, voles, gophers, mice, muskrats, even rabbits and other birds.[^9^]

**How long do they live?**
About 15 years on average; the banding record is 23–25 years. But ~2 of 3 die in their first year.[^10^][^11^]

**Why did one squawk at me?**
You surprised it. The harsh "frawnk" is an alarm call that can run 20 seconds.[^12^]

**Are they dangerous to people?**
No — but never handle one: the lightning-fast bill strike can cause serious injury, and handlers have been struck at the eyes. Call a wildlife rehabilitator for injured birds.[^6^]

**Do they migrate?**
Some do, some don't. Northern and eastern birds head south as water freezes (some to Central America); Pacific coast, Florida, and Galápagos birds stay year-round.[^13^]

**Where do they sleep?**
Communally, usually 5–20 birds in tall trees near water.[^14^]

**Is the white one in Florida a different bird?**
It's the "great white heron" — officially a subspecies of the Great Blue, but with genetic and behavioral evidence for full species status; look for pale legs to rule out Great Egret.[^15^]

## Twenty fun facts

1. The scientific name means "heron heron" — Latin *ardea* + Greek *erōdios*.[^16^]
2. One GPS-tracked heron, "Harper," flew **1,424 miles nonstop over the ocean in 38.6 hours**.[^17^]
3. Herons groom with **powder down** — self-generating feather dust that soaks up fish slime.[^8^]
4. The comb on their middle toe is called a **pectinate claw**.[^18^]
5. In Nova Scotia, **98% of one population's diet was flounder**.[^9^]
6. In Idaho, **24–40% of the diet was voles** — these "water birds" are serious mousers.[^9^]
7. They can swallow fish up to **60 cm (2 ft) long** — and occasionally choke trying.[^9^][^6^]
8. Chicks gain **40× their hatch weight in 8 weeks**.[^3^]
9. Eggs are **pale blue** and fade as incubation progresses.[^5^]
10. Nests grow from 20-inch platforms to **4-foot-wide, 3.5-foot-deep** fortresses over years.[^5^]
11. Colonies can exceed **500 nests**, yet the same birds hunt alone all winter.[^5^]
12. A family of four burns about **2,000 calories a day**.[^3^]
13. Seattle made the Great Blue Heron its **official city bird in 2003**.[^19^]
14. Vancouver's Stanley Park colony has been nesting there **since 1921**.[^3^]
15. Herons sometimes nest on **channel markers, duck blinds, and beaver mounds**.[^5^]
16. Parents incubate through **snowstorms** and have fended off Great Horned Owls on camera.[^20^]
17. The first-hatched chick may bully younger siblings to death — **siblicide** is normal.[^21^]
18. At hatcheries, herons were found to eat **mostly diseased fish** — unintentional quality control.[^8^]
19. They occasionally turn up in **Europe** — one made the British List in 2008.[^22^]
20. The genus *Ardea* is at least **15 million years old**.[^23^]
