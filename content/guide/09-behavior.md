# Great Blue Heron Behavior: Solitary Hunter, Social Breeder

The Great Blue Heron lives a double life. For most of the year it is a loner that defends exclusive fishing grounds; for a few months it becomes a colonist, nesting shoulder-to-shoulder with hundreds of neighbors. Understanding that switch is the key to understanding the species.

## The solitary season

Outside the breeding season, Great Blue Herons forage alone and defend feeding territories against other herons with dramatic threat displays: the defender approaches an intruder with its head thrown back, wings outstretched, and bill pointed skyward. Gulls — and occasionally humans — can be targets of the same maneuver.[^1^] Where food is superabundant, spacing relaxes and birds tolerate company, but the default is one heron, one fishing spot.[^2^] Along one Virginia stream, observers noted a heron staked out roughly every 100 yards.[^3^]

Activity peaks around **dawn and dusk**, though herons feed both day and night, and roost communally at night — typically in groups of 5–20 birds in tall, sturdy trees.[^4^][^5^] They are famously still: a hunting heron may barely move for an hour, conserving energy for a single decisive strike.[^6^]

## The social season

At the colony, the same birds become elaborately social. Males arrive first and claim nest sites, then court passing females with neck-stretching, plume-fluffing, bill-snapping displays and circular flights over the colony.[^7^] Once paired, birds greet each other with ritualized ceremonies — twining necks, clattering bills, exchanging sticks, and performing nest-relief displays with erected plumes.[^1^] Colonies can hold several hundred pairs, with multiple nests in a single tree 100 feet or more off the ground.[^8^]

Herons are **seasonally monogamous**: faithful within a breeding season, but most choose new mates each year, and most males pick a different nest each season.[^9^] Colonies themselves are dynamic — they may persist for 20+ years or relocate after disturbance, and adults often return to nest in the same colony where they were born.[^10^][^11^]

## Defense and danger behavior

Away from nests, adult herons rely on size and a dagger bill that makes them "high-risk" prey; few predators attempt them.[^12^] At nests, though, parents are famously pragmatic: confronted by a Great Horned Owl or eagle, a parent will often stand aside or fly off rather than fight — prioritizing its own survival to breed another day — though exceptions exist, including a filmed case of a heron screaming down an attacking owl until it left.[^13^] When a colony suffers a predation event or repeated human intrusion, the birds may abandon the site entirely, eggs and chicks included.[^12^]

## Quirks worth knowing

- **Bill-clapping**: both sexes snap their bill tips together in territorial and breeding displays.[^14^]
- **Winter scavenging**: Utah herons have learned to steal or scavenge fish from Common Goldeneyes diving in small pockets of open water.[^15^]
- **"Nest hopping"**: large chicks sometimes clamber between neighboring nests in a crowded colony.[^16^]
- **Weather tolerance**: parents sit tight on eggs through late-winter snowstorms; feathers shed rain remarkably well, aided by powder-down grooming.[^17^]
- **Water landings and swims**: though rarely seen, herons can swim and will land briefly on water.[^18^]
