# Great Blue Heron Eggs, Chicks, and Growing Up

## The numbers

| Stage | Detail |
|---|---|
| Clutch size | 2–6 eggs (typically 3–5; average ~4)[^1^][^2^] |
| Egg appearance | Pale blue, fading slightly with age; a little larger than a chicken egg[^1^][^3^] |
| Egg dimensions | 2.4–3.0 in × 1.8–2.0 in (6.1–7.6 × 4.5–5 cm)[^1^] |
| Laying interval | About every 2 days (asynchronous)[^3^][^4^] |
| Incubation | 25–30 days (commonly 27–29), shared by both parents[^1^][^5^] |
| Hatching | Eyes open, covered in pale gray down, able to vocalize[^1^] |
| First flight | ~60 days (range 49–81 days in nest)[^1^][^5^] |
| Independence | ~10–12 weeks; parents and young then part ways[^3^][^4^] |
| First breeding | Not until at least 2 years old[^4^] |
| Broods per year | 1 in the north; sometimes 2 in the south; up to 2 replacement clutches after failure[^4^][^5^] |

## Hatching into a hierarchy

Because eggs are laid at two-day intervals and incubation starts early, chicks hatch **asynchronously** — the oldest may be nearly a week ahead of the youngest. That head start matters: older siblings intimidate younger ones to claim more food, and in lean years the smallest chicks starve, a grim but common outcome known as siblicide.[^3^] In a typical brood, only about **two young survive**; mortality comes from starvation, falls from the nest, predators such as raccoons, and human disturbance.[^4^]

## Feeding the machine

Both parents feed the chicks by **regurgitation** — at first directly into the chicks' open bills, later simply depositing partially digested fish into the nest bottom for the young to pick up.[^4^][^6^] Chicks stimulate feeding by pulling the parent's bill downward.[^3^] Growth is explosive: from about **50 g at hatching to roughly 2 kg at fledging — a 40-fold gain in eight weeks** — and a family of four needs around 2,000 calories a day, keeping both parents foraging nearly nonstop.[^2^] For the first 3–4 weeks one parent broods constantly (typically the male by day, the female by night); after that, both parents hunt.[^2^][^6^]

## Milestones to first flight

- **Weeks 1–4**: downy gray chicks, brooded day and night; fed by regurgitation.[^4^]
- **~Week 6**: chicks reach near adult size.[^3^]
- **~Week 7**: vigorous wing-flapping; hopping branch to branch ("branching").[^6^]
- **~Week 8**: first flights.[^6^]
- **Weeks 10–12**: young disperse; families dissolve — herons do not stay together after the season.[^3^][^6^]

## Watched by millions

The Cornell Lab of Ornithology's nest cam at Sapsucker Woods, New York — streaming since 2012 from a nest 40 feet up a dead white oak that herons have used since 2009 — gave the public an intimate record of this process. In 2012 and 2013 the pair laid five eggs each year; viewers watched the parents incubate through snowstorms, defend against a nighttime Great Horned Owl attack, and raise all eight hatched chicks to fledging.[^7^] The cam documented real-time egg-laying 48 hours apart and the chicks' loud clacking food-begging — details now embedded in the nesting timeline on this page.[^8^]
