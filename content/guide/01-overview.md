# Great Blue Heron Overview: North America's Largest Heron

Whether poised at a river bend or cruising a coastline on slow, deep wingbeats, the Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*) is one of the most familiar and majestic waterbirds on the continent. It is the largest heron in North America — a bird that can stand nearly four feet tall yet weigh barely five or six pounds — and one of the most widespread and adaptable wading birds in the Americas, found from the southeast coast of Alaska to Nova Scotia and south to the Caribbean, Central America, and the Galápagos Islands.[^1^][^2^]

## A bird of superlatives and contradictions

The Great Blue Heron looks prehistoric and behaves like it. It hunts with infinite patience, standing motionless in shallow water for long stretches, then strikes with a lightning-fast thrust of its dagger-like yellow bill.[^1^] Although it is a specialist of wetlands — marshes, riverbanks, estuaries, lake edges, tidal flats — it is also comfortable stalking gophers and voles through dry meadows and farmland, and it can hunt by night as well as by day thanks to eyes rich in rod-type photoreceptors.[^1^][^3^] Most of the year it is a solitary, territorial hunter; in breeding season it crowds into noisy treetop colonies called heronries or rookeries that can hold 500 or more nests.[^4^]

## Key facts at a glance

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Status among herons | Largest heron in North America; worldwide, only the Goliath Heron and White-bellied Heron are larger[^2^] |
| Length / wingspan / weight | 97–137 cm; 167–201 cm; typically 2.1–2.5 kg[^5^] |
| Flight | Neck folded in a tight "S," legs trailing, slow wingbeats; cruises ~20–30 mph[^2^][^6^] |
| Diet | Mainly fish, plus amphibians, reptiles, crustaceans, insects, small mammals, and birds[^7^] |
| Breeding | Colonial nester; 2–6 pale blue eggs; both parents incubate and feed young[^4^][^7^] |
| Longevity | Average ~15 years; banding record 23–25 years; ~67–71% die in their first year[^8^][^9^] |
| Population | ~700,000 globally (Partners in Flight); increasing in the U.S., 1966–2019[^4^] |
| Protection | Migratory Bird Treaty Act; IUCN Least Concern[^4^][^10^] |

## Why this bird matters

Great Blue Herons sit at the top of wetland food webs, and because contaminants biomagnify up the food chain into their tissues and eggs, scientists across North America use them as a sentinel species for ecosystem health — monitoring programs on the St. Lawrence River have tracked mercury, PCBs, DDE, and flame retardants (PBDEs) in heron eggs since 1991.[^11^] Their presence signals productive fish and amphibian populations and functioning wetlands; their colonial nesting habits make them efficient to monitor, and their charisma makes them powerful ambassadors for wetland conservation — a role formalized in Seattle, which named the Great Blue Heron its official city bird in 2003.[^12^]

## Where they stand today

Nationally, the picture is encouraging: the North American Breeding Bird Survey shows numbers stable to increasing between 1966 and 2019, and Partners in Flight rates the species just 8 out of 20 on its Continental Concern Score — a species of low conservation concern.[^4^] But the averages hide real trouble spots. Maine has documented a 31% statewide decline in nesting pairs since 2009 and a 93% collapse in nesting pairs on coastal islands since 1983, with an expanding Bald Eagle population implicated.[^13^] On the Pacific coast, the non-migratory *fannini* subspecies is listed in Canada as a species of Special Concern, with nesting productivity down perhaps 50% since the 1970s.[^14^] The Great Blue Heron's story is thus both a conservation success and a case study in why local monitoring matters.
