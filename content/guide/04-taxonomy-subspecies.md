# Great Blue Heron Taxonomy, Subspecies, and the Great White Heron Debate

## Naming and classification

Carl Linnaeus described the Great Blue Heron in 1758 in the tenth edition of *Systema Naturae*, from a specimen attributed to Hudson Bay.[^1^] The scientific name is a doubled compliment to the bird: *Ardea* is Latin for "heron," and *herodias* comes from the Ancient Greek ἐρῳδιός (*erōdios*) — also meaning "heron."[^2^] It belongs to the order Pelecaniformes and the family Ardeidae, which includes more than 60 species of herons, egrets, and bitterns worldwide.[^3^]

The Great Blue Heron forms a **superspecies** with two close relatives: the Grey Heron (*Ardea cinerea*), which fills the same ecological niche across Eurasia, and the Cocoi Heron (*Ardea cocoi*) of South America.[^4^] Among living herons, only the Goliath Heron of Africa and the White-bellied Heron of the Himalayas are larger.[^4^]

## The five recognized subspecies

| Subspecies | Authority | Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| *A. h. herodias* | Linnaeus, 1758 | Most of North America | The nominate, primarily migratory northern form |
| *A. h. fannini* | Chapman, 1901 | Pacific coast, southern Alaska to Washington | "Pacific Great Blue Heron"; darker, non-migratory; Special Concern in Canada |
| *A. h. wardi* | Ridgway, 1882 | Kansas & Oklahoma to northern Florida | The largest dark subspecies; some authorities doubt its validity |
| *A. h. occidentalis* | Audubon, 1835 | Southern Florida, Caribbean | The all-white "great white heron" (see below) |
| *A. h. cognata* | Bangs, 1903 | Galápagos Islands | Island form[^4^] |

Subspecies boundaries are contested. Up to nine subspecies have been proposed historically; recent reviews recognize anywhere from two to five, with the variation outside the Pacific Northwest sometimes dismissed as clinal.[^5^] The *fannini* population is distinct enough to carry its own Canadian conservation assessment, and *occidentalis* is at the center of a genuine scientific controversy.[^5^][^6^]

## The great white heron: morph, subspecies, or species?

The all-white herons of south Florida and the Keys were described by Audubon in 1835 as a full species, *Ardea occidentalis*. Through the 20th century most check-lists folded them into the Great Blue Heron as a color morph or subspecies, and intermediate "Würdemann's" birds were long interpreted as hybrids.[^7^] The debate never fully died. A 2019 study (McGuire et al.) found that white and dark herons mate **positively assortatively** — whites strongly prefer whites and darks prefer darks — and microsatellite DNA analysis split the birds into two genetic groups, one containing the dark subspecies and one containing Florida Bay *occidentalis* together with Würdemann's-type birds.[^7^] A 2019 paper in the *Journal of Heron Biology and Conservation* argued on this evidence that the Great White Heron should again be treated as a species — noting that Würdemann's birds might represent the *dark morph* of *occidentalis* rather than hybrids.[^7^] Notably, BirdLife International's Red List already assesses "Great White Heron *Ardea occidentalis*" separately — as **Endangered**, last assessed in 2020.[^8^]

For content purposes, the safest phrasing: the American Ornithological Society currently treats the great white heron as a subspecies (*A. h. occidentalis*) of the Great Blue Heron, while some researchers and BirdLife International treat it as a distinct species.[^5^][^8^]

## Deep history

The genus *Ardea* is ancient — the earliest record is an undescribed species from the early Barstovian North American Land Mammal Age (Miocene, roughly 15 million years ago), and the Great Blue Heron itself is an extant species with Pleistocene first appearances in the fossil record.[^1^][^4^]
