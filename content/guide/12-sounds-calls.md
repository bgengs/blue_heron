# Great Blue Heron Sounds and Calls: A Guide to the "Frawnk"

Great Blue Herons are mostly silent birds — stealth is their hunting strategy — but when they do vocalize, the sound is unmistakable: a harsh, hoarse, almost prehistoric squawk that has convinced more than a few listeners they've heard a dinosaur.[^1^][^2^] The voice most people know is the alarm squawk given when a heron is flushed from a shoreline; at breeding colonies, though, the birds are remarkably chatty, with a documented vocabulary of **seven distinct calls**.[^3^]

## The seven-call vocal array

| Call | What it sounds like | When it's used |
|---|---|---|
| **Frawnk** | A rapid, drawn-out squawk averaging ~20 seconds | Alarm and aggression toward other herons, day or night; the source of the New England nickname "Crank"[^3^] |
| **Go-go-go** | A series of clucks | First response to a slowly building disturbance, at feeding sites and colonies; escalates to *Frawnk* if the threat continues[^3^] |
| **Awk** | A startling scream, ~2.3 seconds | Extreme disturbance, e.g., a predator attack[^3^] |
| **Gooo** | Resembles the bleat of a calf | Ends the "Full Forward" courtship display[^3^] |
| **Ee** | A two-segment call | Various social situations, mostly in flight, day and night[^3^] |
| **Roh-roh-roh** | A ~3.6-second series of squawks | Advertises ownership of a feeding territory; also given arriving at the nest — likely functions in mate recognition[^3^] |
| **Landing call** | Similar to *Roh-roh-roh* | Given when arriving at the nest; mate recognition[^3^] |

Descriptions follow Birds of the World (Vennesland & Butler 2020), drawing on R. D. Bayer's colony studies. Calls intergrade and vary considerably between individuals.[^3^]

## How to hear them

The most reliable places to hear Great Blue Herons:

1. **Flush one accidentally** (you will): the departing *frawnk* is the everyday heron sound of North American waterways.[^2^]
2. **Visit a heronry in breeding season**: colonies are loud with greeting squawks, bill-clattering, and the "che-che-che" of chicks (which deepens to "grr-grr-grr" as they grow).[^4^][^5^]
3. **Online**: the Cornell Lab's Macaulay Library hosts extensive recordings, and the All About Birds sounds page features the go-go-go building into a full frawnk.[^6^]

## Beyond the voice

Herons also communicate mechanically: both sexes **snap their bill tips together** ("bill-clappering") during territorial and pair-bond displays, a sound that carries well across a colony.[^7^] Chicks add their own soundtrack — a loud clacking when begging for food, audible in the nest-cam era to millions of viewers.[^8^]
