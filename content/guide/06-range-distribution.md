# Great Blue Heron Range and Distribution: From Alaska to the Galápagos

The Great Blue Heron is one of the most widespread wading birds in the Americas. Its core range covers most of North America — from the southeast coast of Alaska across southern Canada and the entire continental United States — and extends south through Mexico and Central America to the northern coasts of Colombia and Venezuela, throughout the Caribbean, and out to the Galápagos Islands.[^1^][^2^] Remarkably, it is also a confirmed vagrant across the Atlantic: it has been recorded in the Azores and coastal Spain, and in 2008 a bird was admitted to the British List.[^2^][^3^]

## The range at a glance

| Zone | Where | Seasonality |
|---|---|---|
| Breeding range | Alaska (SE), Canada south of the far north, all of the Lower 48, Mexico, Caribbean, Galápagos | Spring–summer; year-round in the far south |
| Year-round range | Pacific coast (Alaska to Washington), southern U.S., Mexico, Central America, Caribbean, Galápagos | Present all year |
| Winter-only range | Southern U.S. through Central America, Caribbean, northern South America | Roughly September–April |
| Vagrant | Azores, Spain, UK, other parts of far southern Europe | Exceptional records[^1^][^2^][^3^] |

## Regional texture

- **Pacific coast**: The *fannini* subspecies occupies the coast from southern Alaska to Washington and does not migrate; British Columbia holds an estimated 5,000–6,000 birds, mostly in the Lower Mainland and southern province.[^4^][^5^]
- **Interior Canada**: Breeding occurs along valley-bottom rivers such as the Kootenay, Columbia, Okanagan, and Thompson, with winter use restricted to ice-free stretches around places like Creston and Castlegar, BC.[^6^]
- **Northeastern U.S.**: Common around beaver ponds, reservoirs, and coastal wetlands; North Carolina's 2012 aerial survey alone tallied more than **7,680 nests in 518 colonies** in the Piedmont and Upper Coastal Plain.[^7^]
- **Florida**: Year-round and abundant; the only place to see all three plumage forms (blue, white "great white heron," and Würdemann's).[^8^]
- **California**: Fairly common year-round in estuaries and fresh and saline wetlands; fewer rookeries in the south, many scattered across the north.[^9^]

## Filling the map: a generalist's strategy

Two traits explain the enormous range. First, dietary flexibility — a bird that can switch between fish, frogs, crabs, mice, and gophers is never far from a meal.[^10^] Second, hardiness: as long as open, fish-bearing water exists, herons can overwinter far north of where most people expect them, including frozen-edge rivers in Utah and New England.[^11^][^12^] The northern boundary of year-round distribution is drawn not by cold but by **ice**: when water freezes solid and snow blankets the uplands, herons must move.[^11^]

## Range dynamics

The footprint is not static. Breeding Bird Survey data show the species increased in the U.S. between 1966 and 2019, and Audubon's Survival by Degrees climate modeling rates the Great Blue Heron "Stable" under warming — projecting about 36% range gained, 100% of current range maintained, and 0% lost at +1.5 °C.[^13^][^14^] Colonization of created habitats — reservoirs, beaver ponds, aquaculture districts, urban parks — has driven much of the 20th-century expansion, as in North Carolina's interior and British Columbia's interior valleys.[^6^][^7^]
