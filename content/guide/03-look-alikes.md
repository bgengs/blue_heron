# Great Blue Heron Look-alikes: Heron vs. Egret vs. Crane and More

The Great Blue Heron's size and shape make it nearly unmistakable across most of North America, but a handful of species — and one famous color morph — regularly cause confusion. This page collects the comparisons people search for most.

## The 60-second rule set

| If you see… | It's probably a… |
|---|---|
| Huge blue-gray wader with a heavy yellow bill | Great Blue Heron |
| Large all-white wader with **black legs** and a slim yellow bill | Great Egret |
| Large all-white wader with **pale/yellowish legs** and a heavy bill (south Florida only) | "Great White Heron" (white Great Blue) |
| Big gray bird flying with its **neck straight out** | Sandhill Crane (not a heron) |
| Small dark heron, crow-to-goose sized | Little Blue Heron or Green Heron |
| White-necked, black-crowned giant (South America overlap) | Cocoi Heron |

## Great Blue Heron vs. Great Egret

This is the classic confusion, especially in Florida where the all-white "great white heron" morph lives. The Great Egret is snow-white, noticeably smaller and more slender, and — the clincher — has **black legs and feet**; the white-morph Great Blue Heron is larger and heavier-billed with **yellowish or greenish legs**.[^1^][^2^] The Great Blue stands taller and is roughly twice as heavy as a Great Egret.[^3^] In flight both fold their necks, but the Great Blue's bulkier frame and slower, more labored wingbeats stand out.

## Great Blue Heron vs. Sandhill Crane

Herons and cranes aren't close relatives, but their silhouettes invite mix-ups. In flight the difference is absolute: **herons fly with the neck folded into an "S"; cranes fly with the neck extended straight out**.[^2^] Other distinctions: Sandhill Cranes have a crimson crown patch, shorter bills, only three toes (they can't perch in trees), eat mostly tubers and vegetation rather than fish, gather in flocks of thousands, and yodel loudly in flight — while herons are near-silent and usually travel alone or in small groups.[^4^]

| Trait | Great Blue Heron | Sandhill Crane |
|---|---|---|
| Neck in flight | Folded "S" | Stretched straight |
| Diet | Fish, amphibians, small animals | Tubers, grains, vegetation |
| Nesting | Trees, often colonial | Ground in shallow wetlands, solitary pairs |
| Social | Solitary hunter | Huge migratory flocks |
| Voice | Harsh squawks | Rolling, yodeling calls |
| Toes | 4 (can perch) | 3 (cannot perch)[^4^] |

## Great Blue Heron vs. other herons

- **Little Blue Heron** — much smaller (56–74 cm long, under 0.5 kg), with a two-toned blue-gray-based bill and no white on the head; juveniles are all-white and can be confused with egrets.[^5^]
- **Reddish Egret & Tricolored Heron** — far smaller, coastal, with distinct proportions and colors; both lack the Great Blue's white-headed, black-striped pattern.[^3^]
- **Grey Heron** (*Ardea cinerea*) — the Great Blue's Old World ecological twin; a rare vagrant to North America's northeast coasts. It is somewhat smaller with a uniformly pale gray neck, lacking the Great Blue's rusty tones.[^3^]
- **Cocoi Heron** — overlaps in northern South America; distinguished by a striking white neck and solid black crown (dull juveniles are the tricky ones).[^3^]
- **Sandhill Crane, Wood Stork, American White Pelican** — all large and long-legged, but none combines the folded-neck flight, shaggy plumes, and dagger bill of a Great Blue.

## The trickiest case: Würdemann's and the "Great White Heron"

Within south Florida and the Keys, Great Blue Herons occur in three plumage types: the standard blue-gray form; the entirely white **great white heron** (subspecies *occidentalis*); and the intermediate **Würdemann's heron** — a grayish body with a mostly white head and neck.[^6^][^7^] Birders visiting Florida should learn all three; the white birds are routinely misreported as Great Egrets, and Würdemann's birds baffle even experienced observers.
