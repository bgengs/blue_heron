# Watching Great Blue Herons: Where, When, and How

## The good news: they're findable almost everywhere

Great Blue Herons live year-round across most of the United States and southern Canada anywhere there is fish-bearing water — shorelines, riverbanks, marsh and pond edges, estuaries, even drainage ditches and golf-course ponds.[^1^] Scan shallow water for a tall, statue-still gray figure; look up for the slow-winged, neck-tucked silhouette.[^2^] Dawn and dusk are peak activity, and because hunting herons stand in the open and stay still, they are among the easiest large birds to observe and photograph.[^3^][^4^]

## Famous places to see them

| Site | Why go | Best time |
|---|---|---|
| **Venice Area Audubon Rookery, Florida** | A tiny island rookery in a stormwater pond, protected by resident alligators; Great Blues plus ~12 other wading species at close range; covered pavilion; free | Nesting Dec–May; early morning or late afternoon; docents with scopes Jan–Apr[^5^] |
| **Stanley Park, Vancouver, BC** | Urban colony documented since 1921 (100+ nests) beside a major park; interpretive program and Adopt-a-Nest | Return mid-Feb–Mar; chicks Apr–May[^6^] |
| **Commodore Park / Kiwanis Ravine, Seattle** | Seattle's largest colony of its official city bird, in red alders by the Ballard Locks | Activity Feb–late Jul; biannual public heron watches[^7^] |
| **Union Bay Natural Area, Seattle** | Top urban birding wetland; year-round birds | Year-round[^8^] |
| **Sapsucker Woods, Ithaca, NY** | Cornell Lab headquarters; the famous nest cam nest (since 2009) in a pond-side dead oak | Herons return late Mar; nesting Apr–Jul[^9^] |
| **Bear River Migratory Bird Refuge, Utah** | Year-round herons, including winter birds fishing ice-edge pockets | Year-round, incl. winter[^10^] |
| **Crow's Nest Natural Area Preserve / Potomac Creek Heronry, Virginia** | Large monitored heronry; annual nest counts with conservation partners | Spring nesting[^11^] |
| **Great Blue Heron Nature Reserve, Chilliwack, BC** | A reserve named for the bird, with trails through heron habitat | Spring–summer[^12^] |
| **Chincoteague NWR, Virginia** | Coastal marsh birding; herons staked out along channels roughly every hundred yards | Year-round[^13^] |

Any local eBird hotspot with wetland habitat will do — herons are among the most reported birds on the continent.

## Watching the breeding season responsibly

Heronries are spectacular but fragile. Follow these rules and you'll be welcome back:

1. **Keep 100 m (330 ft) or more** from active nests; use binoculars or a long lens instead of approaching.[^14^]
2. **Never use drones** near colonies (banned at Venice Rookery and broadly harmful).[^5^]
3. **Keep noise down** — loud or unusual sounds cause abandonment, especially before eggs hatch.[^14^]
4. **Leave pets at home**; stay on marked trails and respect fences (they protect birds *and* people from falling sticks and guano).[^6^]
5. Visit in the **early morning** for the most activity and best light.[^5^]

## Photography notes

The Venice Rookery has become an international photography mecca precisely because nests sit near eye level across a narrow pond — modest lenses work.[^15^] Everywhere else, herons reward patience differently: a motionless hunting bird lets you compose carefully, and the strike — if you're ready — is the shot of the day. Flight shots are easiest at colony flight lines at dawn and dusk, when birds commute between rookery and feeding grounds.[^4^]

## Seasonal cheat sheet

| Season | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Winter | Northern birds at ice-free rivers and coasts; field-hunting rodents; goldeneye scavenging in Utah[^10^] |
| Spring | Colony return (Feb–Mar), courtship displays, stick exchanges, nest repair[^6^] |
| Summer | Chicks, feeding flights, fledging (Jun–Aug); dispersing juveniles (dark-crowned) everywhere[^4^] |
| Fall | Migration overhead (Sep–Nov, small groups), southbound birds on any shoreline[^16^] |
