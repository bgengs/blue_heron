# Chapter 3: Evolution of the Herons

The great blue heron stands at the edge of a marsh, motionless, its neck folded into a compressed S. The posture looks ancient. In one sense it is. The family Ardeidae — herons, egrets, and bitterns — has occupied shallow-water habitats for tens of millions of years. Fossil bones assignable to the family appear in rocks from the early Oligocene, roughly 33 million years ago, and fragmentary remains hint at an earlier Eocene origin. But the bird at the marsh edge is not a relic unchanged since that time. It is a modern species, *Ardea herodias*, shaped by extinction, migration, and slow modification. The lineage is old. The species is not.

## Bones in the Rock

The fossil record of herons is sparse. Heron bones are thin-walled and hollow — adaptations that lighten the body for flight but rarely survive burial. What persists is fragmentary: a partial humerus, a few vertebrae, the coracoid of a shoulder. From such pieces paleontologists must decide whether a bird belonged to Ardeidae or to some related lineage that converged on the same body plan.

The earliest temporally well-constrained fossil assigned to the family comes from the Borgloon Formation of Belgium. Described by Gerald Mayr and Thierry Smith in 2019, in *Ibis*, the specimen — named *Proardea? deschutteri* — dates to the lowermost Oligocene, approximately 32 to 33 million years ago. The question mark signals uncertainty: the fragmentary remains share features with herons but cannot be placed with full confidence. An earlier species, *Proardea amissa* — the "lost proto-heron" — is sometimes attributed to the late Eocene, though its age and referral remain debated.

An even older candidate, *Calcardea junnei*, was described in 1987 from the late Paleocene of Wyoming and initially assigned to Ardeidae. If correct, it would push the family's origin back nearly 30 million years. But in 2019, Mayr, Philip Gingerich, and Smith reexamined the holotype and rejected the heron identification, concluding that *Calcardea* more closely resembled *Vastanavis*, an early Eocene bird from India unrelated to modern herons. A partial skeleton can resemble a heron without being a heron. Convergence — unrelated lineages evolving similar forms independently — is a constant hazard.

## The Eocene-Oligocene Turning Point

The appearance of *Proardea? deschutteri* in early Oligocene Belgium follows one of the most significant climate transitions of the Cenozoic. The Eocene-Oligocene transition, centered around 34 million years ago, marked the shift from a largely ice-free greenhouse world to an icehouse climate, with the first major glaciation of Antarctica and sustained global cooling. Terrestrial faunas underwent turnover as forests contracted.

Mayr and Smith noted that paleontological data suggest herons arrived in Europe shortly after this turnover. The reshuffling opened ecological space. Shallow wetlands — river margins, lake edges, floodplains — expand and contract with climate. When the transition reorganized landscapes, it may have created opportunities for wading birds already possessing the basic tool kit: long legs, long necks, and pointed bills. Whether the family originated before the transition and becomes visible after it, or diversified in response to it, remains unresolved.

By the Early Miocene, herons had reached the Southern Hemisphere. A fossil heron from the Bannockburn Formation at St. Bathans, New Zealand — based on seven bones — documents the family in the South Pacific. By the Miocene, herons were already a worldwide family.

## The Architecture of the Neck

The feature that most distinguishes a heron from a crane or a stork is the neck. Herons possess 20 or 21 cervical vertebrae — far more than the 7 of mammals. Modified articulations among the fifth through seventh vertebrae create a characteristic kink, allowing the neck to fold into a tight S-shape. In flight, herons retract the neck against the body, unlike storks and cranes, which extend the neck forward.

The same architecture enables the strike. A heron hunting in shallow water stands or walks slowly, then drives its bill forward by rapidly straightening the folded neck. The coiled S acts as a spring, and one elongated mid-neck vertebra appears to serve as a hinge. A 2020 study in *Integrative Organismal Biology* examined cervical morphology across 24 species, focusing on Ardeidae and Anhingidae (darters). Ardeids and anhingas — both ambush predators that strike by straightening the neck — share specialized mid-neck vertebrae, suggesting the morphology evolved with this feeding mode. Whether this reflects common ancestry or convergence remains open.

The great blue heron's neck is ancient in principle and specific in execution. A foldable neck suited to ambush predation in shallow water is likely as old as the family. The precise articulation in *Ardea herodias*, tuned to its body and prey, is later refinement.

## Legs, Bills, and the Shallow-Water Niche

Long legs and a dagger-like bill are the other signatures of the heron body plan. Long legs allow a bird to wade where a short-legged species cannot, reaching prey in water deep enough to deter terrestrial predators but shallow enough that fish and amphibians remain visible. The bill — straight, sharp, and heavy in the great blue heron — functions as spear and forceps.

These features recur across many species, suggesting a stable ecological demand. The great blue heron represents one extreme: large body, long legs, heavy bill. Smaller herons — the green heron, the least bittern — occupy the same niche at a different scale. The body plan scales to the habitat.

Not every heron feature is equally ancient. The pectinate claw — a comb-like serration on the middle toe — is shared across the family and manages powder down, feathers that produce a talc-like substance used to condition plumage. Whether these appeared early or evolved later is not known. Powder down does not fossilize.

## Lineage and Species

It is essential to distinguish between an ancient lineage and a modern species. The genus *Ardea* — large herons including the gray heron of Eurasia and the great blue heron of the Americas — has a more recent history than the family, though its exact age is debated. In 2023, Hruska and colleagues published a phylogeny of Ardeidae based on ultraconserved elements and mitochondrial DNA, sampling 55 species. The study resolved relationships that morphological data alone could not, but the timing of divergences remains uncertain, partly because the sparse fossil record provides few calibration points.

The great blue heron, *Ardea herodias*, was described by Linnaeus in 1758, but is far older than its name. Fossil remains attributable to the species are known from Pleistocene deposits in North America, indicating the bird was present during the last ice age, tens of thousands of years ago. It has not been traced back millions of years.

The species is not uniform. *Ardea herodias fannini*, the Pacific great blue heron, is a non-migratory subspecies on the coast from Washington to Alaska, partly isolated by mountain ranges. *Ardea herodias occidentalis*, the great white heron of southern Florida and the Caribbean, is entirely white and has been debated as a potential full species — it interbreeds with the nominate form in Florida Bay, so most authorities retain it as a subspecies. These forms show that *Ardea herodias* is still differentiating.

## What Remains Unknown

The history of herons is written in fragments. The exact origin of Ardeidae is unknown. The Eocene candidates are uncertain; the Oligocene record is better constrained but incomplete. Relationships among herons, ibises, and other waterbird families have shifted repeatedly as molecular data has accumulated. Whether the shared neck morphology of herons and anhingas reflects common ancestry or convergence is unresolved. The fossil record offers nothing about soft tissues, plumage, or behavior.

What the evidence supports is a measured claim. Herons have been herons — long-legged, long-necked, shallow-water hunters with foldable necks and pointed bills — for at least 33 million years and probably longer. The great blue heron is one branch of that lineage, shaped by North American wetlands and still sorting itself into regional forms. It carries an ancient architecture in a modern body.

---

A bird that has stood at the water's edge for tens of millions of years earns gravity, but gravity is not immortality. The lineage survived the Eocene-Oligocene cooling, the Miocene warm intervals, and the Pleistocene ice ages — not because it was unchanging, but because it changed enough. The great blue heron we watch today is the current answer to an old question: how does a bird make a living in shallow water? The answer has been revised countless times, across a changing Earth. That the revision continues — in the isolation of *fannini*, in the white plumage of *occidentalis* — is not a flaw. It is the design.

### Heron Fact

Herons have 20 or 21 cervical vertebrae — more than three times the number in a human neck. Modified articulations among the fifth through seventh vertebrae create the S-shaped fold. Unlike storks and cranes, herons retract the neck in flight. The coiled posture that compresses the neck for travel stores the energy for the strike. One elongated mid-neck vertebra appears to act as the hinge.

### Myth or Fact?

**Myth:** The great blue heron has existed unchanged for millions of years, a living fossil.

**Fact:** The family Ardeidae is ancient — the earliest well-constrained fossil, *Proardea? deschutteri*, dates to roughly 33 million years ago — but the great blue heron, *Ardea herodias*, is a modern species. It is known from Pleistocene fossils in North America, likely tens of thousands of years old, but has not been traced to the Oligocene. The lineage is old; the species is not. Older candidates once called herons, such as *Calcardea junnei* from the Paleocene, have been reinterpreted as unrelated birds.
