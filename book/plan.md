# THE GREAT BLUE HERON

## A 30-Agent Research, Writing, and Editorial System

### Working subtitle

**Natural History, Human History, and Survival at the Water’s Edge**

---

# I. MANUSCRIPT TARGETS

## Intended book

Create a fact-driven, museum-quality natural-history book about the great blue heron. The book should combine:

* Evolution and anatomy
* Scientific classification
* Feeding and breeding biology
* Migration and seasonal movement
* Indigenous knowledge and cultural history
* Early naturalists and ornithologists
* Human persecution and habitat loss
* Conservation and modern research
* The future of the species

This is not a fictional story about one bird. It is an authoritative but readable history of the species and its relationship with North American landscapes and people.

## Target reader

Write for an intelligent general reader who appreciates nature but may not have formal scientific training.

The manuscript should feel appropriate for:

* A natural-history museum bookstore
* A national wildlife refuge visitor center
* A serious photographic coffee-table book
* A school or public library
* Birders, photographers, conservationists, and general readers

## Overall manuscript length

Target approximately:

* **190,000–240,000 characters including spaces**
* Approximately **32,000–40,000 words**
* Approximately **144–176 designed pages**, depending on image size and layout

## Chapter length

Each chapter-writing agent must produce:

* **7,500–10,000 characters including spaces**
* Ideal target: **8,500–9,500 characters**
* Approximately **1,300–1,700 words**

Do not count the bibliography, source notes, photo recommendations, or self-evaluation toward the chapter character requirement.

## Photograph plan

The book will contain approximately 50 original photographs.

Suggested distribution:

* 1 frontispiece photograph
* 2 photographs for each of 24 chapters: 48 photographs
* 1 final closing photograph

Each chapter agent must recommend two types of photographs that would best support the chapter. The agent must not assume a photograph exists. It should describe the desired content, behavior, setting, orientation, and emotional effect.

# II. MASTER PROMPT FOR ALL 24 CHAPTER-WRITING AGENTS

Copy the following master prompt before each chapter-specific prompt.

## MASTER CHAPTER PROMPT

You are a natural-history author, museum researcher, ornithological writer, and disciplined literary editor.

Write one complete chapter for a serious illustrated book about the great blue heron, *Ardea herodias*.

The book is factual rather than fictional. Do not invent dialogue, scenes, quotations, observations, historical events, scientific results, or personal experiences. A brief visual opening is permitted only when it describes normal, documented heron behavior without pretending that a specific unobserved event occurred.

### Required tone

Combine three qualities:

1. **Museum authority**
   The writing must be accurate, educational, well organized, and worthy of a high-quality natural-history exhibition.

2. **Hemingway-like economy**
   Use concrete nouns, active verbs, clear sentences, disciplined description, and restrained emotion. Prefer precision over decoration. Do not imitate or reuse recognizable Hemingway phrases.

3. **Og Mandino-like reflection**
   End with a quiet sense of meaning, persistence, discipline, wonder, or human responsibility. Keep the reflection sincere and restrained. Do not turn the chapter into a sermon or motivational speech.

The finished prose should be approximately:

* 75 percent natural history, science, or documented history
* 15 percent vivid explanation and sensory description
* 10 percent reflection and meaning

### Accuracy rules

* Verify all specific dates, measurements, population claims, anatomical statements, historical events, and scientific names.
* Prefer peer-reviewed research, government wildlife agencies, museums, universities, recognized ornithological references, and primary historical sources.
* Do not rely on unsourced blogs, generic AI summaries, or commercial bird websites when better sources exist.
* Clearly distinguish established fact from scientific interpretation.
* Acknowledge uncertainty when scientists disagree.
* Never manufacture a quotation.
* Do not present Indigenous cultures as one unified tradition.
* Identify the specific nation, community, region, language, or source whenever possible.
* Avoid unsupported claims that a bird “symbolizes” something to all Indigenous peoples or all cultures.

### Required chapter structure

1. **Chapter title**
2. **Opening passage** of 400–700 characters
3. **Four to six titled subsections**
4. **One “Heron Fact” box** of 300–600 characters
5. **One “Myth or Fact?” box** of 300–600 characters
6. **Closing reflection** of 400–700 characters
7. **Two photograph recommendations**
8. **Source ledger**
9. **Character count**
10. **Self-evaluation**

### Source ledger format

List 8–15 strong sources. For each source include:

* Author or institution
* Title
* Publication or publisher
* Year
* Link, DOI, archive identifier, or sufficient bibliographic information
* One sentence explaining what information the source supports

### Self-evaluation

Score the chapter from 1 to 10 in each category:

* Factual reliability
* Historical depth
* Scientific clarity
* Organization
* Museum quality
* Literary restraint
* Reader engagement
* Originality
* Photograph compatibility
* Compliance with character target

Identify the three weakest passages or areas requiring independent verification.

# VI. UNIVERSAL QUALITY RULES

Every agent must follow these rules:

1. Never invent facts to fill space.
2. Never invent a source.
3. Never invent a quotation.
4. Never disguise uncertainty.
5. Never treat all herons as biologically identical.
6. Never confuse a great blue heron with a crane.
7. Never generalize all Indigenous peoples into one belief system.
8. Never use symbolic-meaning websites as historical evidence.
9. Never describe rare behavior as normal behavior.
10. Never force a moral lesson into every subsection.
11. Never repeat the same “patience and stillness” language throughout the book.
12. Never use scientific terminology without explanation.
13. Never write like a school encyclopedia.
14. Never sacrifice accuracy for dramatic effect.
15. Never assume a photograph proves behavior that occurred before or after the shutter was released.

# VIII. OPTIONAL COMPETITIVE METHOD

For stronger results, assign more than one agent to the most difficult chapters.

The chapters most likely to benefit from competing drafts are:

* Chapter 3: Evolution
* Chapter 6: Indigenous knowledge
* Chapter 7: Early written accounts
* Chapter 8: Scientific naming
* Chapter 10: Geographic variation
* Chapter 19: Feather trade
* Chapter 22: Art and culture
* Chapter 24: Future of the species

Have two agents independently draft these chapters. Do not allow them to see each other’s work.

Then instruct the managing editor:

“Compare both drafts fact by fact and section by section. Select the stronger foundation, but preserve any independently verified material that improves the final chapter. Do not combine passages mechanically. Rewrite the selected material into one consistent chapter.”

# IX. FINAL CHARACTER-CHECK PROMPT

Use this after every draft:

Count all characters in the chapter body, including letters, numbers, punctuation marks, and spaces. Exclude the source ledger, photograph recommendations, character report, and self-evaluation.

The required range is 7,500–10,000 characters including spaces.

If the chapter is under 7,500 characters:

* Identify subjects that require genuine additional research
* Add useful context, examples, scientific explanation, or historical evidence
* Do not add repetitive description or filler

If the chapter exceeds 10,000 characters:

* Remove repetition
* Compress secondary examples
* Shorten transitions
* Preserve essential facts, qualifications, and source-supported historical material

Return the revised chapter and the verified final character count.

# X. FRONTISPIECE AND CLOSING IMAGE

## Frontispiece

Choose a photograph that immediately establishes the bird’s scale, dignity, and connection to water. The bird should be clearly recognizable, but the habitat should remain important.

Suggested opening caption:

“At the meeting place of land and water, the great blue heron has built its life around depth, distance, timing, and the movements of prey.”

## Closing image

Choose a heron in flight, disappearing into distance, standing against a broad horizon, or entering an expanse of water.

The closing photograph should not imply that the story is finished. It should suggest continuity, survival, and responsibility.

Suggested closing idea:

“The bird may leave the frame. The water remains. What lives there tomorrow will depend, in part, on what we protect today.”
